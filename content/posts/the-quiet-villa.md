There is a particular hush that settles over the house just before dusk. The concrete, warmed all afternoon by a stubborn Mediterranean sun, gives back its heat in slow exhalations. Floor-to-ceiling glass dissolves the boundary between the great room and the valley beyond, and for a moment — perhaps two — the architecture seems to hold its breath.

## A House That Begins with the Site

"I wanted nothing in front of the view," the architect tells me, gesturing toward the unbroken horizon. "Everything else followed from that single sentence." The result is a long, low volume pressed into the ridge, its only ornament the play of cantilevers against sky.

Inside, the palette is restrained to the point of austerity: board-formed concrete, smoked oak, a single pale travertine slab in the kitchen. Furniture has been chosen with a collector's eye — a Pierre Jeanneret armchair here, a quiet Castiglioni floor lamp there — but never permitted to perform.

## The Discipline of Restraint

What lingers, after one walks the rooms, is not any single object but the editing. Every surface has been argued for. Every joint has been drawn twice. The villa is, in the end, less a house than an essay on what one can do without.

> "Luxury, properly understood, is the absence of the unnecessary."

By nightfall, the only light comes from a recessed cove washing the ceiling and the slow blue of the pool below. The architect pours wine. The valley darkens. The house, finally, falls silent.
